Class Journal

We will keep track of what we cover in class each day through this journal. It won't contain any of the nitty-gritty details of what we have done in a given class, but will instead serve to summarize the key theorems we proved or discussions we had.

Week 1

  • Tuesday, 2.1.11: Dana briefly summarized the structure of the course. We then discussed the natural numbers, integers, and the definition of divides. We also discussed the basic assumptions that we can make for this course, such as the closure of the integers under addition and multiplication and transitivity of $\leq$. We finished by proving an example theorem dealing with the definition of divides.
  • Thursday, 2.2.11: We discussed the Elements of Style for Proofs handout and the began our first round of student presentations. All of the presentations were great, especially given that it was our first go. AS proved Theorem 1.1, IL proved Theorem 1.2, RC proved Theorem 1.3, and AM proved Theorem 1.6.

Week 2

  • Tuesday, 2.8.11: KH presented a proof of Theorem 1.11, EC proved Theorem 1.12, CQ tackled Theorem 1.14, and CM presented a proof of Theorem 1.15. After the student presentations, Dana briefly discussed induction and the division algorithm.
  • Thursday, 2.10.11: Great class! RC nailed 1.17, MW proved 1.18, NT presented a proof of the difficult 1.26, and KH and JC tackled 1.27, which we sorted out collectively.

Week 3

  • Tuesday, 2.15.11: As a class, we quickly discussed 1.29, 1.30, and 1.31. IL and AM tackled 1.28, each doing one direction of proof. AS beat out CQ for 1.32 and presented a flawless proof. KH showed us an impressive sketch of the argument for 1.33, which is the key ingredient to the Euclidean Algorithm, which we discussed as a class.
  • Thursday, 2.17.11: At the beginning of class, Dana briefly discussed Diophantine equations and then we dived into student presentations. AM and RC computed some gcds using the Euclidean Algorithm for 1.36. MW reversed engineered the work that AM did to produce a solution to a Diophantine equation. JC presented the difficult .138, and we finished with BP proving 1.39. We left 1.40 open.

Week 4

  • Tuesday, 2.22.11: QR, BP, JK, and EC simultaneously wrote up proofs to 1.40, 1.41, 1.42, and 1.43, respectively. NT presented 1.45. With the time we had left toward the end of class, Dana attempted to summarize what we've done so far.
  • Thursday, 2.24.11: We started class by eating pizza! Then Dana continued with summarizing Chapter 1. RC1 and AC proved each direction of 1.48 and ZG presented 1.51.

Week 5

  • Tuesday, 3.1.11: AS tackled 2.1, RC2 provided an informal proof of 2.7 while CM presented a sketch of a more formal proof, RC1 and CQ attempted 2.8 (but this one remains open), and CM came up a second time and presented 2.9 (which needed some minor adjustments to work).
  • Thursday, 3.3.11: AS presented the forward direction of 2.12 while AM proved the reverse direction. RC1 provided a sketch for 2.13, CQ/KD presented 2.19, and EC quickly proved 2.21 using 2.19.

Week 6

  • Tuesday, 3.8.11: JC attempted an "iff" proof for 2.26, but we decided that it was more trouble that it was worth. RC2 presented the contrapositive of the forward direction of 2.26. BP showed us a flawless proof of 2.27 (Euclid's Lemma). Both IL and RC1 presented wonderful proofs of 2.32. KH attempted 2.33, but realized a flaw. JC came to the board and acted as scribe for an argument that we collectively came up with for 2.33. We finished by discussing a proof of 2.34, which essentially tells us that there are infinitely many primes.
  • Thursday, 3.3.11: We discussed the assignment for Weekly Write-Up 5, which requires students to peer review two proofs written by students from Wellesley College. Then Dana discussed a few open problems related to Mersenne and twin primes. We also discussed the Goldbach Conjecture. We finished class by allowing students to work on the take-home exam.

Week 7

  • Tuesday, 3.13.11: Dr. Maatta covered class. IL tackled 2.34 while KH and JC made contributions to 2.46. RC1 made an attempt on 2.38, but the problem remains open.
  • Thursday, 3.15.11: No class.

Week 9

  • Tuesday, 3.29.11: Dana did a little bit of lecturing today. We discussed the big picture of each of 3.14, 3.15, 3.16, and 3.17. We also discussed the definition of complete residue system and canonical complete residue system. In addition, CM presented a proof to 2.38, which is probably the most difficult proof we've encountered so far.
